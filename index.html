Ohio Third Frontier · Ohio Department of Development

What is TVSF

A two-phase commercialization grant program run by Ohio Third Frontier

Quarterly cycles. Reviewed by Redwood Innovation Partners (Columbus). Active since April 2012.

Phase 1 — Institutional

Pools awarded to Ohio research institutions (universities, hospitals, federal labs) to fund proof-of-concept on unlicensed IP.

Three sub-options ranging from $100K to $1M+. Recipients spend 12 months running internal validation projects designed to prepare technologies for startup licensing.

Phase 2 — Startup

Direct grants to Ohio startups commercializing IP licensed from a research institution.

Cap rose from $150K to $200K starting Round 35 (Jan 2024), with up to 20% allowed for personnel. Used for prototype development, pilot deployments, and market validation.

8 · Notable outcomes

Pattern shifts

Federal-lab IP is now structural, not exceptional

Roughly a third of Phase 2 awardees license from federal labs — most funneled through the Entrepreneurs' Center (Dayton).

Federal labs as IP source

Air Force Research Laboratory · Naval Surface Warfare Centers (Crane, Dahlgren, Carderock) · Naval Postgraduate School · Army DEVCOM (Armaments / CBC / Soldier Systems) · ERDC · NSA · NASA Ames · NASA Glenn · VA

Mostly Active companies with traction: Raider Tech, Dayton Photonics, Parcell, Hyphen, Vitruvian.

Sector mix shifting

Historical baseline: ~55% Biomedical/Life Sciences. Recent rounds: R38 = 21% biomed (lowest in dataset, 36% Software/IT). R39: 36% biomed.

Evaluators flag this every round, recommending outreach to underrepresented Third Frontier focus areas (Energy, Sensors, Adv. Manufacturing).

Reapplication strategy works

Many funded companies were previously rejected: Neuright, Raider Tech, Rescue Ventilation, Altered Gravity, Spectrin, Afference, DEB Tech, Volynt, ComHealth, WhichFoodsR, Auxilium, Tactical Triage, Snow Troopers.

Counter-example: A&I Multifunctional Materials needed three tries; CrossLims rejected twice.

12 · Northwest Ohio

Northwest Ohio · Observations

A small footprint with structural causes — and a few bright spots

Six findings from the NW Ohio dataset — a diagnosis of why the regional share is small and what would change it.

01 · Doubly under-represented

3.6% of applications, 2% of dollars

Only 7 of 196 program-wide applications came from NW Ohio. Of those, 3 were funded — a 43% acceptance rate vs. the 58% portfolio average. Total $500K out of $24.9M deployed.

02 · Concentrated in 2 rounds

R35 and R44 — nothing in between

All NW Ohio funding flowed in just two rounds: R35 (Jan 2024, $400K to UToledo) and R44 (Apr 2026, $100K to BGSU). Eight consecutive rounds R36–R43 saw zero NW Ohio awards — a ~2-year drought.

03 · Sector tunnel vision

6 of 7 applications are Biomed/Life Sciences

No therapeutics. No diagnostics. No SaaS. No sensors. No federal-lab-licensed startups. Avani/Plastinuva (Advanced Materials, plastic recycling) is the only diversifier across all 14 rounds.

04 · Funded startups are thriving

100% Active — but value is leaking

Both R35 awardees (Altered Gravity, Avani/Plastinuva) still Active 2+ years post-grant — outperforming the 74% portfolio average. However: Avani rebranded to Plastinuva and relocated to Akron Bounce. The standout success now sits in NE Ohio.

05 · ESP infrastructure gap

No Toledo-area equivalent of Rev1 / EC / JumpStart

BG Biologics flagged "no ESP." Cardinex got "limited help from UToledo Business Incubator." Cleveland has Bounce/JumpStart, Columbus has Rev1, Dayton has the Entrepreneurs' Center, Cincinnati has UC's 1819 Venture Lab. NW Ohio lacks comparable commercialization muscle.

06 · Phase 1 pipeline = BGSU only

UToledo has never applied as a Phase 1 grantee

Despite being the IP source for 5 of 7 NW Ohio Phase 2 applications, the University of Toledo has never sought Phase 1 institutional funding in this window. BGSU's R44 award is the only NW Ohio Phase 1 grant — and a first-time application.

14 · Watch list

Persistent evaluation gaps

Business Model and Management Team are the lowest-rated criteria — every round

IP Protection, Proof of Concept, and Growth-Plan-in-Ohio are consistently the strongest. The pattern is so consistent it's now a structural recommendation.

Strongest categories (every round)

  • IP Protection — usually 80%+ "meets or exceeds"
  • Proof of Concept — strong customer/data validation
  • Growth Plan in Ohio — applicants tightly aligned with state job/growth requirements
  • Project Plan / Budget — typically clean and 12-month-aligned

Weakest categories (every round)

  • Business Model — Round 26 hit 28% "meets or exceeds"; rolling avg ~64%
  • Management Team — frequently lacks business/fundraising experience
  • Investor / Partner Engagement — many founders haven't lined up downstream capital
  • ESP Interaction — lack of engagement with Entrepreneurs' Service Providers is a recurring red flag

Standing Redwood recommendation: applicants should add business/fundraising experience to technically-strong founder rosters and engage their ESP earlier in proposal development.

15 · Evaluation gaps

The data gap

These reports don't measure outcomes — they're evaluator deliverables

The reports describe who got funded and why. They tell you almost nothing about whether funded companies subsequently succeeded.

What the reports cover well

  • Per-round funding amounts and approval rates
  • Awardee/applicant lists with project descriptions
  • Technology source institutions
  • Sector breakdowns and cumulative program totals
  • Reapplication and Phase 1 carry-through tracking

What's missing

  • Post-grant company performance data
  • Follow-on funding raised by awardees
  • Job creation, revenue, or commercialization milestones
  • Failure / dormancy / dissolution tracking
  • Phase 1 → Phase 2 → market progression metrics

UARF's self-reported $21M raised across 15 licenses with 2 exits is the only substantive follow-on metric in 14 reports.

Bottom line

A professionally-run, sector-broadening, federal-lab-friendly grant program

TVSF funds at the ~$200K-per-startup tier — small grants by VC standards, but meaningful for the proof-of-concept work that de-risks subsequent fundraising.

Recent rounds show deliberate scale-up via institutional Phase 1 grants (R38, R44) — ODOD is investing in the upstream pipeline as much as in any single company. The portfolio is healthy on the surface (74% Active, 1 acquisition, ~$30M follow-on), but the cohort is too young to confirm ROI. Revisit Round 31 awardees in 2028+ for a defensible answer.
